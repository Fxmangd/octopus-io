State-of-the-Art Performance: Octopus outperforms all competing rehearsal-free and rehearsal-based methods by a substantial margin.

The Challenge: Sequentially learning tasks while mitigating catastrophic forgetting.

Abstract

Continual learning in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) aims to sequentially acquire knowledge while mitigating catastrophic forgetting, yet existing methods face inherent limitations: architecture-based approaches incur additional computational overhead and generalize poorly, rehearsal-based methods rely on storing historical data (raising privacy concerns), and conventional regularization is insufficient to fully prevent parameter interference.

We propose Octopus, a two-stage continual learning framework based on History-Free Gradient Orthogonalization (HiFGO), which enforces gradient-level orthogonality without historical task data. Our proposed two-stage finetuning strategy decouples task adaptation from regularization, achieving a principled balance between plasticity and stability. Experiments on UCIT show that Octopus establishes state-of-the-art performance, surpassing prior SOTA by 2.14% and 6.82% in terms of Avg and Last performance.

Key Contributions:

  • HiFGO: A novel gradient orthogonalization mechanism that relies solely on past weights, making it incredibly data-efficient and privacy-preserving.
  • Two-Stage Fine-Tuning: A paradigm that allows models to explore optimal solutions freely before enforcing constraints, perfectly balancing plasticity (learning new info) and stability (retaining old info).
  • Rehearsal-Free SOTA: Achieves unprecedented performance on the UCIT benchmark without needing to actively store and replay data from prior tasks.

The Challenge & The Octopus Solution

Stage 1: Unconstrained Adaptation

During the first fine-tuning stage, both regularization terms are deactivated. This allows the MLLM to freely adapt and approach a local optimal region for the current task, maximizing learning plasticity.

Stage 2: HiFGO Constraints

In the second stage, we apply History-Free Gradient Orthogonalization. By bounding parameter updates orthogonal to the Gradients of Previous parameters Within Current data (GPWC), we prevent interference with old knowledge. Crucially, this uses only past weights—no historical data is needed!
