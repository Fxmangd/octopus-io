Abstract

Continual learning in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) aims to sequentially acquire knowledge for new vision-language tasks while preventing catastrophic forgetting. Traditional methods face a critical dilemma: architecture-based approaches endlessly increase parameters resulting in computational overhead, while rehearsal-based methods rely heavily on historical data, which raises privacy concerns and storage expenses.

To address these limitations, we propose Octopus, a novel continual learning framework based on History-Free Gradient Orthogonalization (HiFGO). By mathematically leveraging Gradients of Previous parameters Within Current data (GPWC) as an accurate proxy, Octopus efficiently identifies and protects sensitive optimization manifolds of older tasks without requiring any historical data. Combined with a customized two-stage finetuning strategy, Octopus elegantly balances plasticity and stability. Extensive experiments on the UCIT benchmark demonstrate that Octopus achieves state-of-the-art performance, surpassing even rehearsal-based baselines.

Why GPWC Works?

The fundamental insight of HiFGO lies in calculating the gradient sensitivity of historical tasks to steer current optimization. Under strict privacy constraints without old data, we propose GPWC (Gradients of Previous parameters Within Current data distribution).

Theoretical Proof: GPWC is mathematically equivalent to the projection of the previous task's Hessian matrix onto the tangent space of the current task's data manifold.

Figure 3. Validity of the HiFGO constraint. Utilizing GPWC successfully restores prior task performance compared to standard sequential fine-tuning.

Figure 4. Comparison of the quadratic form of parameter increment. GPWC guides model updates precisely into low-curvature flat regions of the old tasks.

Two-Stage Finetuning

Stage 1: Unconstrained Adaptation

Inspired by simulated annealing, the model first freely finetunes on the new task data. This phase temporarily ignores stability to maximize plasticity, allowing the model to quickly absorb new feature representations and plunge into the local minimum optimal for the new distributions.

Stage 2: HiFGO Constraints

In the second stage, we activate the HiFGO constraint. This regularized tuning efficiently "pulls back" parameters onto a safe subspace that minimizes interference with the previously learned knowledge, acting as a crucial consolidation step for stability.

Experimental Results

Comprehensive Performance Comparison

Evaluations on the demanding multi-task UCIT benchmark. Octopus significantly outperforms recent baselines (such as O-LoRA and HiDe-LLaVA) on Average (Avg) and Last performance metrics, achieving SOTA results without requiring the storage or rehearsal of past data.

Backward Transfer (BWT) Analysis

A critical indicator of continual learning capability is Backward Transfer. Instead of suffering from negative transfer, Octopus effectively extracts generalized representations from new tasks that are highly compatible with historical knowledge, yielding an exceptional positive backward transfer (BWT = +0.41).

Robustness and Hyperparameter Sensitivity

Extensive sensitivity analyses confirm that Octopus maintains consistently optimal performance across a broad spectrum of parameter settings and randomized non-stationary task orders.

Inference Efficiency

Compared to MoE-based strategies that require loading numerous redundant parameter experts as task sequences grow, Octopus utilizes a unified structure, introducing zero additional computational footprint during the inference phase.
